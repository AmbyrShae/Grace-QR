“As late as 1976, a 500 pound life sized bronze water nymph mysteriously disappeared from her base on Swan Island in the front lagoon, the victim, perhaps, of thieves, or of pranksters who may have lost the dead weight in the black of night. Its fate, like its origin, is still unknown.”

Historic City Park New Orleans

As a child, I lived on Sherwood Forest Drive a block from City Park. I grew up looking at an empty pedestal. It captured my imagination and I wondered what had been there. The pedestal was still there when I moved back into the neighborhood 35 years later.

A couple of years ago I was approached by Michael Manjarris to participate in Sculpture for New Orleans, a program which is responsible for the art on the Poydras corridor as well as various sites around the city. My first thought was of the empty pedestal. I wanted to replace what had been lost but instead of telling a story of an abduction I thought to change the story into one in which the girl left the island of her own volition, traveled the world and, after four decades of exploration, realized there is no place she’d rather be than mid-city, the heart of New Orleans.

I proposed to create a six foot bronze sculpture of the woman the girl became but wanted to reference the original statue. I contacted the museum and City Park but neither had records or images of the little girl. I spent hours researching at the Historic New Orleans Collection where I found a book called Sculpture in New Orleans that cataloged all of the statues around town. The center two-page spread had three photographs of female statues in which the caption read: “The statues in Audubon Park are beautiful.” I knew two of the statues: one in the fountain at the front of the park facing Tulane and Hygia and the other the fountain in Audubon Zoo. The third picture was of a little girl with a hand outstretched. I knew that was the girl but needed confirmation.

During my research I interviewed neighbors who remembered the statue. One said it had a water feature -- that the water came out of her hand. I asked Barry Kaiser, a photographer I work with if he remembered the theft. He said he remembered it but did not have a photo of the original statue. However, he knew someone who did. I contacted his friend, Owen Murphy, who sent me a photo of a statue of a little girl with her hand outstretched- it was the same statue as the one in the book inaccurately labeled as being in Audubon Park.

The piece, called Grace, is a female form that demonstrates strength through adversity as well as how we are shaped by our experiences and the power of home.
